Context

Jamaican agriculture runs more on intuition than on data. Surveying is largely manual and slow, field boundaries and conditions are poorly documented, and the island's hurricane exposure means the shape and state of the land can change in a single season. The farmers who most need current spatial data are the least able to buy it one survey at a time.

The Jamaica Agricultural Society (JAS) engaged JDMS to close that gap: bring drone-based photogrammetry to its member farmers and cooperatives, and do it in a way that communities would accept and local institutions could sustain. My role was to design and run the delivery program: the training pipeline, the parish rollout, the stakeholder structure, and the data operation behind it.

The wedge: agriculture is the worst segment to enter first, fragmented across thousands of small buyers and highly price-sensitive. The answer was institutional. JAS aggregates that fragmented demand into a single anchor client with island-wide reach, and its legitimacy opens doors no startup could open alone. The wedge was not a sector; it was a partner.

Stakeholder structure

The hardest part of the program was building a two-tier public-sector stakeholder structure that let a drone program land in rural communities without friction, and repeating that structure in every parish.

StakeholderTierRole in the program
Jamaica Agricultural SocietyPrimaryAnchor client and institutional partner: program requirements, farmer relationships, land access, and the legitimacy that made the program credible island-wide. Each parish pilot program was co-designed with JAS.
Town aldermenSecondaryLocal authority and community buy-in in every parish. Each pilot program was structured with its aldermen as named secondary stakeholders, so drones arrived with local government endorsement rather than as an outside imposition.
Local governments & cooperativesPartnership network180 partnerships coordinating site access, scheduling, and community participation, the connective tissue between the flight schedule and the communities beneath it.
Trained pilotsDelivery workforce120 local operators, recruited and trained through the program, executing the mapping initiatives in their own parishes.

Authority: none of these stakeholders reported to the program. Every parish rollout depended on aligning an institutional client, elected local officials, cooperative leadership, and a newly trained workforce around one delivery plan, 10 times over.

Key tradeoffs, as delivered

Each call traded speed, cost, or control against durability, and each call is visible in the delivery record above.

DecisionThe tensionThe call made
Train local pilots vs. import expert crewsA small expert crew flies sooner; a training pipeline delays first flights by 90 days.Train local. 120 parish-resident pilots produced coverage, community trust, and lasting capacity that a visiting crew never could. The 90-day investment is why the program scaled to 10 parishes.
Photogrammetry vs. LiDARLiDAR offers superior precision; photogrammetry offers lower equipment cost per pilot and a skill set trainable at scale.Photogrammetry. It matched agricultural accuracy needs, kept per-pilot equipment cost viable at 120 operators, and could genuinely be taught in 90 days. Don't pay for precision the use case won't fund.
Distributed parish programs vs. centralized operationsA central operation is easier to quality-control; distributed programs risk inconsistent data.Distribute the people, standardize the pipeline. Parish programs kept delivery local while the DJI-to-DroneDeploy-to-ArcGIS pipeline enforced one capture and processing standard everywhere.
Institutional anchor vs. direct-to-farmer salesSelling farm by farm means thousands of small, price-sensitive buyers; a single anchor concentrates dependency in one relationship.Anchor on JAS. One institutional client aggregated the fragmented demand, and the alderman tier de-risked the dependency by rooting each parish program in its own local authority.

Outcomes

  • Delivery volume: 83 mapping initiatives completed through one standardized pipeline, from flight plan to ArcGIS deliverable.
  • Geographic coverage: operational pilot programs in 10 parishes, not a single demonstration site.
  • Capability transfer: 120 trained local pilots who remain in their communities, a workforce the island keeps after any single contract ends.
  • Institutional network: 180 local government and cooperative partnerships, the durable relationship layer future mapping work builds on.

The lasting outcome is the combination: data, workforce, and relationships accumulating together. Each mapping initiative made the next one cheaper to deliver and easier to get permission for.

Where this goes: from surveys to a national data layer

JDMS should not remain a survey vendor paid per flight. Every paid mapping initiative is also a deposit into a proprietary national dataset, which means the services business is funding the asset.

  • Phase 1 - delivered services as a data-acquisition engine. The 83 initiatives above built the first layer of coverage while paying for themselves.
  • Phase 2 - the dataset becomes the moat. Once coverage and freshness pass a threshold, the accumulated data is worth more than any single flight. A competitor can buy the same drones; they cannot replicate years of current coverage, 120 trained pilots, and 180 standing partnerships overnight.
  • Phase 3 - recurring access. The unit of value shifts from "a survey" to a maintained, freshness-guaranteed data layer for a defined area, opening adjacent segments (disaster response, land titling, infrastructure monitoring) that build on data agriculture already paid to collect.

The north-star metric for that transition: square kilometers of current, accurate, accessible data. "Current" and "accurate" are the load-bearing words; they are what a subscriber would actually be paying for.

Where this fits: the drone is only the collection mechanism; the asset is the dataset. The delivered program proved the collection engine works at national scale. The platform is the sequel, and it only exists because the wedge was real.

Key takeaways

  • Influence without authority: aligning an institutional client, elected officials, cooperatives, and a new workforce around one delivery plan, repeated across 10 parishes.
  • Capability building as delivery strategy: training 120 local pilots was slower to start and faster to scale, and it left the capacity behind.
  • Standardization at scale: with distributed operators, the data pipeline, not supervision, is what guarantees consistent output.
  • Wedge through an aggregator: a fragmented market becomes enterable when one institution aggregates its demand and lends its legitimacy.
  • Delivery funds strategy: the services engagement was run as a data-acquisition investment, so the completed work doubles as the foundation for the platform thesis.

If I could only defend one decision: training 120 local pilots instead of importing expert crews. It cost 90 days before the first initiative flew, and it is the single reason the program reached 10 parishes, earned 180 partnerships, and left Jamaica with a mapping workforce it did not have before.
