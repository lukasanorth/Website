Jamaica Drone Mapping Services (JDMS) - From Survey Vendor to National Data Platform

Sequencing a drone-mapping startup from one-off projects into a recurring geospatial product for Jamaica. Strategy, prioritization, and roadmap reasoning from zero to first recurring revenue.

Role
Product Manager
Company
Jamaica Drone Mapping Services
Constraint
~$700K runway
Scope
Strategy · Prioritization · Roadmap

The problem

Jamaica is one of the most hurricane-exposed places on earth, and almost every consequential decision the island makes depends on knowing the shape of the ground beneath it. Yet the foundational layer of geographic truth is weak: surveying is largely manual and slow, land boundaries and property records are fragmented, and agriculture runs more on intuition than on data.

The pattern is the same across sectors: a decision-maker needs accurate, current spatial data, and the only options are expensive, late, or both. A disaster-management official can't wait weeks for a manual survey of a flooded parish. A land-registration department can't resolve a boundary dispute without an authoritative map. A farmer can't optimize planting without knowing the contours and condition of the field.

Callout: the opportunity is not "do surveys faster." It is to own the authoritative geospatial data layer for an entire country, map it once, keep it current, and let many sectors build on top of it. The drone is just the collection mechanism. The asset is the dataset.

Users & segments

Four customer groups all "want maps," but their jobs-to-be-done, urgency, willingness to pay, and buying processes diverge sharply. Treating them as one market would have been the first mistake.

SegmentJob to be doneUrgencyBuyer notes

Land & propertyEstablish authoritative digital boundaries for titling and taxationMediumSlow procurement, high accuracy bar, but very durable demand once won
Civil infrastructureSurvey sites, monitor build progress, verify against designMedium-highProject-based and repeatable; price-sensitive but data-literate
AgricultureMap fields, monitor crop health, optimize inputsLow-mediumFragmented, many small buyers; cost is the gating factor

Where to start

With roughly $700K of runway, JDMS could not serve four segments well at launch. The first real product decision was choosing a wedge in the single segment that would prove willingness to pay, generate reference cases, and produce reusable data. I would lead with disaster planning, for three reasons:

  • Urgency creates a budget. Hurricane response is time-critical and politically visible, so a public buyer will pay for speed manual surveying can't match.
  • It produces the highest-value data. Disaster work demands LiDAR-grade precision that also serves infrastructure and land titling later the data compounds in value for the next buyer.
  • A single anchor buyer shortens the sales motion. One credible government reference unlocks the rest of the public sector faster than chasing fragmented agricultural demand.

Agriculture, despite the largest count of potential customers, is deliberately deferred: it is the most fragmented, the most price-sensitive, and the lowest-precision, a poor place to learn whether the core thesis holds.

The core bet: services that become a platform

Read as product strategy, the arc is a deliberate services-to-product transition:

  • Phase 1: services as a data-acquisition engine. Every paid mapping project is also a deposit into a proprietary national dataset. We get paid to build the asset.
  • Phase 2: the dataset becomes the moat. Once coverage and freshness pass a threshold, the dataset is more valuable than any single flight. A competitor can buy the same drones; they cannot replicate years of accumulated, current coverage overnight.
  • Phase 3: recurring access and analytics. The unit of value shifts from "a survey" to "ongoing access to current truth," plus predictions on top: flood-risk modeling, yield forecasting, infrastructure-need detection.

The hard pricing question: what exactly is the recurring unit of value for buyers used to paying once? My answer: a maintained, version-controlled data layer for a defined area, with freshness guarantees not a one-time file. That reframing is what justifies recurring revenue.

Scoping the MVP

"Map the entire island" is a classic v1 trap: it front-loads enormous capital before any market signal. The first release should be the smallest thing that validates the thesis:

  • Smallest mappable scope: one or two high-risk parishes rather than national coverage operationally real, small enough to fund and finish.
  • Single segment: the disaster-planning wedge, with one anchor government client.
  • One sharp deliverable: fast, accurate post-event damage and flood assessment that demonstrably beats manual surveying on time and accuracy.

The pilots are the MVP, and their job is to answer one question: will a real buyer pay for drone-derived data over a traditional survey and trust it?

Success metrics

The source plan measures only revenue a lagging outcome. A product needs the leading indicators that cause it, tracked in tiers:

TierMetricsWhy it matters
North starSquare kilometers of current, accurate, accessible dataThe single number capturing whether the asset not just the service is growing. "Current" and "accurate" are load-bearing words.
Leading / productCoverage %, data freshness, accuracy vs. ground truth, time-from-flight-to-deliverable, segment activationThese cause revenue. Freshness and accuracy are what a buyer actually pays for; cycle time is the edge over manual survey.
Platform (Phase 3)Subscription retention, net revenue retention / expansion, share of revenue that is recurringThe real test of the services-to-product transition. Rising recurring share is proof the bet worked.
GuardrailsFlight safety incidents, unit cost per km², regulatory compliance statusGrowth that ignores safety, cost, or regulators is fragile. These keep the north star honest.

Reflection: what makes this a product problem

It would be easy to read JDMS as a business plan and stop there. What makes it a product case study is the transition hiding inside it: the move from selling effort (flights and surveys) to selling an asset (a maintained, trusted national dataset) and eventually intelligence (analytics on top of that data).

The product work is not the drones. It is the sequencing choosing a wedge that funds the asset, deferring the tempting-but-premature pieces (full coverage, AI, agriculture), and defining the recurring unit of value precisely enough that customers pay for it on repeat. Get the sequence wrong and you have an expensive survey shop. Get it right and you have the data layer a country builds on.

If I could only defend one decision: treat every early services engagement as a data-acquisition investment, and refuse to ship the platform until the dataset is genuinely worth subscribing to. Everything else follows from that discipline.
