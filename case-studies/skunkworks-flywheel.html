Project Skunkworks: Flywheel Adoption Accelerator

Turning resistant senior marketers into change champions. A behavioral change management initiative designed to drive adoption of new marketing execution tools at American Express.

This conceptual initiative addresses the gap between technological advancement and organizational adoption by engaging senior marketers as active participants in the change process.

Role
Director-level strategic proposal
Organization
American Express - U.S. Consumer Marketing

Overview

Enterprise technology adoption remains one of the most persistent challenges in organizational change management. Even when new systems offer demonstrable performance advantages, adoption stagnates, not because employees lack capability, but because they lack trust, motivation, and emotional investment in the transition. The tension is most acute among senior practitioners who built their expertise on legacy tools.

Rather than relying on top-down mandates or passive training, this program recruits senior, change-resistant marketing leaders as pilot participants and invites them to lead experimentation within the new system. Through a structured seven-phase program combining experimental campaigns, structured reflection, and public recognition, the initiative converts institutional skeptics into internal champions.

The goal isn't to push change on them, it's to let them experience the value of the new systems firsthand, build their confidence through creation, and then model that behavior for the rest of the organization.

The problem

American Express's marketing organization is staffed with seasoned professionals who have refined their craft over years or decades. That institutional knowledge is a competitive asset and a source of inertia when transformational change is introduced. Three root causes of adoption resistance were identified in this cohort:

  • Lack of trust in new tools, often stemming from prior failed rollouts or incomplete onboarding experiences.
  • Inability to see the performance upside, due to insufficient data or poor demonstration of the system's capabilities.
  • Absence of emotional investment, because change was being done to them rather than by them.

Standard change management approaches training sessions, adoption dashboards, executive communications had proven insufficient for this segment. A fundamentally different strategy was required.

Theoretical framework

The program design draws on four established bodies of research in organizational behavior:

  • Experiential learning (Kolb, 1984). Concrete experience (experimental campaigns), reflective observation (After-Action Reviews), abstract conceptualization (playbook development), and active experimentation (real-world deployment) reinforce each other in a loop.
  • Diffusion of innovations (Rogers, 2003). By targeting senior, influential marketers first, a segment typically classified as "late majority", the program inverts the conventional diffusion sequence. Respected institutional voices generate social proof no communication campaign can match.
  • Self-determination theory (Deci & Ryan, 1985). Participants get autonomy (they choose their experiments), mastery (structured learning and iteration), and purpose (visible organizational impact).
  • The flywheel effect (Collins, 2001). Each cohort rotation reduces friction, builds trust, and amplifies adoption speed for the broader organization.

Program structure

Seven sequential phases, spanning roughly five to six months per cohort rotation:

PhaseDurationDescription
1. Training & Onboarding2 weeksTargeted enablement on new marketing systems with just-in-time learning materials and live support.
2. Experimental Project4–6 weeksParticipants select passion-driven mini-campaigns. Focus: creativity within new system constraints.
3. After-Action Review1 weekStructured debrief: what worked, what didn't, what can improve. Captures operational and emotional feedback.
4. Celebration & Recognition1 weekWins highlighted publicly in newsletters, town halls, and Flywheel Friday. Reinforces momentum.
5. Real-World Deployment6–8 weeksLearnings applied to actual market-facing campaigns under real business constraints.
6. Final AAR + Executive Review1 weekEvaluates outcomes, time savings, and adoption metrics. Insights presented to leadership.
7. Scale / RotateOngoingExpands to additional regions or rotates new members to diffuse adoption culture.

Two design choices matter most. First, experimentation is deliberately sequenced before real-world deployment, giving participants a protected space to fail, learn, and iterate before their work meets live market conditions. Second, campaigns in the experimental phase are selected by participants themselves, based on personal relevance rather than assigned briefs. Ownership of the creative direction is a psychological lever: when practitioners work on something that matters to them, their engagement with the tools is intrinsically motivated rather than compliance-driven.

Governance & incentive design

Four mechanisms balance accountability with psychological safety:

  • Mild deterrence for regression. Teams that revert to legacy processes forfeit access to budget automation tools and campaign visibility dashboards; a meaningful but non-punitive cost, framed as natural consequence rather than discipline.
  • Recognition & ambassador status. Successful participants are publicly recognized as Flywheel Ambassadors across internal channels and future training programs.
  • Closed-loop feedback integration. Insights from Skunkworks experimentation feed directly into platform updates, transforming participants from passive users into co-designers of the system.
  • Radical transparency. A weekly dashboard shared across Marketing Operations tracks time-to-launch, issue resolution rates, and campaign velocity in real time.

Risks & mitigations

The program carries low technical risk but moderate cultural risk:

  • Adoption risk. If participants revert to legacy tools or produce poor results, the signal to the broader organization may be that the new system is unreliable. Senior disengagement carries outsized reputational cost.
  • Operational risk. Pilots may consume disproportionate resources; leadership perception of an "extra project" would undermine sponsorship.
  • Cultural risk. Poorly framed penalties may register as punitive; an elite cohort dynamic could create exclusivity perceptions that reduce cross-team trust.

Seven anticipated roadblocks were each paired with a mitigation; among them, framing the pilot as adoption ROI insurance to secure leadership buy-in, dual-path processes where compliance mandates legacy use, a dedicated program analyst for weekly insight synthesis to fight feedback lag, and positioning participants as founders of a new organizational paradigm to defuse cultural resistance.

Strategic implications

  • Invert the rollout sequence. When the resistant segment holds institutional power and social capital, converting them first produces disproportionate adoption leverage.
  • Reframe, don't enforce. The same individual who resists being changed is often willing to lead change when invited to do so. The Skunkworks model operationalizes this with selective recruitment, elevated autonomy, visible recognition, and direct feedback integration.
  • Close the loop between user and designer. The AAR cadence paired with feedback integration into platform development creates a learning architecture rare in enterprise adoption contexts.

The Skunkworks model carries low technical risk but high cultural reward if managed with transparency and strong communication.

Its success depends on three interlocking conditions: clear metrics and dashboards that make progress visible, psychological framing that feels empowering rather than coercive, and fast feedback integration that shows participants their experience shapes the system they're adopting. Met together, they model an approach that treats resistance as a resource rather than an obstacle, and experience as an asset rather than a liability.
