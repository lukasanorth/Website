Poppin: Closing a 3-5% Revenue Leak and Recovering $4.7MM

Support and replacement orders were consuming 3-5% of total bookings, millions in lost revenue, and even business partners were raising reliability concerns. This program made the supply chain observable, attacked the leak end to end with a cross-functional team of 10, recovered $4.7MM in freight costs from LTL partners, and converted reliability into a selling point that helped grow B2B sales 15%.

Context

Support and replacement orders at Poppin accounted for 3-5% of total bookings, representing millions in lost revenue. The losses stemmed from product defects, transit damage, missing shipments, and warranty replacements, and the problem was visible enough that external business partners were reporting rising concerns about product reliability. Beyond the direct cost, the leak threatened customer satisfaction and long-term business relationships.

I was tasked with leading the initiative to close it: reduce support costs and order counts, shorten resolution times, improve cost recovery, establish a standardized claims process, and build reporting mechanisms with 3PL and installation partners to capture issues earlier in the customer journey. The program unfolded in three acts, because the mandate was earned in stages.

Phase 1 · Making pod damage observable

The sharpest expression of the leak was the privacy pod line. Pods were arriving with damaged panels, rust, damp internals, broken glass, and electrical issues at an unknown or unseen damage rate of 1 in 7, with returns up roughly 8% and replacements up roughly 15%. The expensive part was not any single damaged pod; it was that nobody could say where in the lifecycle the damage was happening, which meant every fix was a guess and every claim against a freight partner was unprovable.

  • QR checkpoint tracking: designed and rolled out QR-based logging of pod condition at each landing point in the supply chain, in real time, so every damage report carried a location and a stage.
  • Adoption across landing points: partnered with operations teams to train staff on scanning procedures, since a checkpoint that doesn't scan is a blind spot.
  • NetSuite-layered dashboards: integrated tracking data into a database layered on NetSuite, feeding live dashboards for visualization and reporting.
  • Stage-level analysis framework: established a framework for analyzing damages by supply chain stage, enabling root cause identification and targeted improvement.

Field reality the design had to absorb: freight partners lacked the personnel to inspect every shipment, and pods were sometimes misplaced entirely; in one case a damage report traced a lot number to a dock where units had sat outside in the elements for days. The system had to produce evidence in an environment where the parties handling the product could not be relied on to self-report.

What the data showed

Once checkpoints were live, the damage stopped being random and started having an address. The distribution concentrated in transit:

Supply chain stageShare of damagesPrimary cause / implication
Ocean freight40%Water ingress in transit: rust, damp internals, electrical failures. Pointed to containerization and packaging interventions.
LTL transit20%Handling damage between warehouse and client site, and the basis for freight claims against LTL partners.
Warehousing10%Storage and handling conditions at facilities, including units left exposed on docks.
Manufacturer3%Units shipping damaged from origin, a small but fully recoverable share.

The remaining share was distributed across other touchpoints or could not be attributed to a single stage. The headline finding: transit, not manufacturing, was the problem, with 60% of attributed damage occurring on the water or on the truck. The pod program alone produced $1.4M in savings, a 10% reduction in replacement costs, a 5% reduction in pod support orders, and a 15% reduction in customer complaints. More importantly, it proved the model: instrument the journey, and the failures identify themselves.

Phase 2 · Scaling to the end-to-end journey

With the pod wedge proven, the mandate expanded from one product line to the full customer journey across the catalog. I led a cross-functional team of 10 to design and implement efficiency initiatives across tracking, analytics, and visualization:

  • Repeat-offender SKU tracking: built systems to track manufacturing defects, warranty replacements, and transit damages for SKUs with repeated support orders, extending the pod methodology to the whole line.
  • Root cause partnership: worked with operations and product teams to pinpoint the causes behind mis-ships, short ships, missing pieces, and factory defects, so fixes landed at the source rather than the symptom.
  • Earlier signal capture: built reporting mechanisms with 3PL and installation partners so issues surfaced earlier in the customer journey instead of at the support ticket.
  • Automation tooling: developed a PM assignment tool that balanced workloads by auto-assigning projects based on dashboard capacity, and a problem-solving tool to log, assign, and escalate recurring product and customer issues against resolution benchmarks.
  • B2B alignment: collaborated with B2B stakeholders to connect analytics, marketing, and sales tools to the new tracking systems, extending end-to-end visibility beyond operations.
  • Fulfillment quality: improved accuracy and reliability through enhanced quality checks, rigorous inspection processes, and closer 3PL collaboration.

Phase 3 · Recovery

The stage-level data changed what was possible. Damage and misplacement during transit was no longer an anecdote; it was documented, timestamped, and attributable. That evidence base justified standing up a dedicated claims and recovery team, which I established and trained, to pursue damaged-pod costs from the LTL partners, the effort that recovered $4.7MM in freight costs.

The recovery capability was built to outlast the initial push:

  • Real-time claims notifications: reduced financial leakage by flagging transit damage as it was logged, improving cost recovery rates across support orders instead of chasing claims after windows closed.
  • Cross-functional ownership: the claims and recovery team spanned operations, product, and CX, so accountability for damage costs had a permanent owner rather than a one-time project.
  • Planning and Category Management channel: a new partnership ensuring parts availability for frequently failing components, with a dropship option for when direct fulfillment beat the standard chain.
  • Recurring governance: a semi-annual partner meeting cadence to proactively surface issues, track trends, and drive accountability with 3PL and installation partners.
  • Leadership visibility: new analytics dashboards giving leadership comprehensive sight of support order trends and operational risks.

The commercial turn: the same transparency built to stop losses became a sales asset. B2B stakeholders used the tracking systems and reliability record as proof points in client discussions, contributing to a 15% increase in B2B sales. An operations program ended up on the revenue side of the ledger, twice: once by recovering costs, once by winning deals.

Method: Diagnose → Build → Adopt → Sustain

This program is the cleanest expression of the operating method that runs through my delivery work:

  • Diagnose: quantify the leak (3-5% of total bookings; a 1-in-7 pod damage rate) and refuse to act until failures had an address, which the stage-level data provided.
  • Build: the QR checkpoint system, the NetSuite-layered database and dashboards, the SKU-level defect tracking, and the automation tools for assignment and escalation.
  • Adopt: training at every landing point, root-cause partnership with operations and product, partner reporting mechanisms, and B2B alignment, because an unscanned checkpoint is a blind spot and an unused tool is shelfware.
  • Sustain: the claims and recovery team, the Planning and Category Management channel, semi-annual partner governance, and leadership dashboards, so the gains survive the person who built them.

Key takeaways

  • Visibility precedes recovery: $4.7MM was recoverable only after damage had documented, stage-level attribution. Evidence is the asset; claims are the harvest.
  • Wedges earn mandates: the pod program's measurable wins are what turned a product-line fix into an end-to-end journey program with a team of 10.
  • Design for the weakest link: the system assumed partners could not inspect shipments, produced its evidence independently, and only then built partner reporting on top.
  • Data compounds across uses: the same instrumentation drove prevention, recovery, planning, workload automation, and ultimately sales enablement, 5 returns on 1 investment in visibility.
  • Reliability is a revenue story: operational transparency became a B2B selling point worth 15% sales growth, proof that supply chain work belongs in commercial conversations.
  • Institutionalize the win: standing teams, partner governance, and leadership dashboards converted a recovery project into a durable operating capability.

If I could only defend one decision: instrumenting the supply chain before attempting any fix. It felt slower in the moment, and it is the only reason a damage problem produced a $4.7MM recovery, a smaller leak, and a sales argument, instead of another round of guesses.
